# How to find underserved software niches

Finding an underserved software niche is not about brainstorming clever ideas. It is about locating jobs that many people already do, where the existing tools fail them, and that you can realistically reach. This guide lays out a repeatable method for doing that, the way Speculora is designed to support.

For the concepts behind each step, see the [gap gradient](gap-gradient.md) and the [opportunity score](opportunity-score.md). To work through a live version, use [speculora.com/gaps](https://speculora.com/gaps).

---

## The core principle

An underserved niche is the overlap of three conditions:

1. **Real demand** — people already do this job and care about it.
2. **Weak supply** — the available tools are missing, bad, or overpriced.
3. **Reachable users** — you can get in front of these people without a budget.

Miss any one and it is not a niche worth your time. A reachable, weakly-supplied job that nobody actually does is a dead end. A high-demand, weakly-supplied job whose users you can never find is someone else's opportunity, not yours.

---

## Step 1 — Start where you have an edge

Pick a domain you genuinely understand or use. Your own recurring frustrations are a free, high-quality demand signal, and domain familiarity lets you tell a real gap from a superficial one and reach users credibly.

Speculora's six domains — [Money & Finance](domains/money-and-finance.md), [Productivity & Organization](domains/productivity-and-organization.md), [Health & Fitness](domains/health-and-fitness.md), [Photo & Video](domains/photo-and-video.md), [Education & Learning](domains/education-and-learning.md), and [Travel & Local](domains/travel-and-local.md) — are a good starting frame.

## Step 2 — Drop from category to task

"Productivity" is not a niche; it is an ocean. Break the domain into **tasks** — discrete jobs like "merge two PDFs on a phone" or "track recurring subscriptions before they renew." Niches live at the task level, because that is where you can actually measure demand and supply. If you cannot state the job in one concrete sentence, you are still too high up.

## Step 3 — Confirm demand before anything else

Before judging supply, confirm people actually want the job done. The strongest evidence is **revealed demand**: people already using tools for it and complaining about them. If a task has a steady stream of real users voicing frustration, demand is proven. Silence is not opportunity — it is usually the absence of demand.

## Step 4 — Read the supply state, not just the count

Now look at what exists. Do not stop at "there are competitors." Place the task on the gap gradient:

- Are users **missing a feature** they keep requesting? (Lacks Features)
- Do the apps have the features but **work badly**? (Poorly Executed)
- Is the job solved but **priced out of reach** for a segment? (Too Expensive)
- Are there **few or weak** players? (Undersupplied)

Any of these is an underserved niche even if dozens of apps technically exist. A crowded market of bad apps is more open than it looks.

## Step 5 — Hunt for the Prime Gap

The best niches are **Prime Gaps**: heavily used tasks that are done badly. Sort your candidates for high demand *and* high dissatisfaction. These beat both fully-served markets (no room) and empty greenfield (no proof). When you find a job thousands of people do and loudly hate the tools for, you have found the prize.

## Step 6 — Test reachability honestly

Ask: how would the first hundred users find my product, for free?

- Do these users **search** for a solution? (SEO and app-store search are open.)
- Do they **gather** anywhere findable? (Communities, forums.)
- Will they **discover** it by browsing app-store categories?

If the only realistic path is paid acquisition against entrenched incumbents, downgrade the niche regardless of how good the gap is. **Distribution, not the idea, is the usual constraint for solo builders** — choose niches you can reach.

## Step 7 — Define the wedge

A wedge is the single sharpest reason to switch. Derive it from the loudest supply-state failure:

- Lacks Features → "the one that finally does X."
- Poorly Executed → "the one that actually works."
- Too Expensive → "the one you can afford."
- Undersupplied → "the one that exists and is competent."

Lead with the wedge. You can broaden later; you cannot enter without a point.

## Step 8 — Move while the gap is fresh

Gaps decay. Once demand is visibly validated, other builders notice it too, and incumbents can close their own gaps by shipping the missing feature. A niche is a time-sensitive asset. If you have found a fresh Prime Gap you can reach, the right next move is to start building, not to keep researching indefinitely.

---

## Common mistakes

- **Chasing empty markets.** "No app exists" usually means no paying demand, not hidden treasure.
- **Counting competitors instead of reading them.** Many hated apps equal soft competition.
- **Ignoring reachability.** A perfect gap you cannot reach is not your opportunity.
- **Picking a domain you do not understand** just because the gap looked big from outside.
- **Researching forever.** Gaps decay; analysis past the point of clarity is just delay.

---

## A quick checklist

- [ ] Stated as a concrete task, not a category.
- [ ] Demand confirmed from real, current user signal.
- [ ] Supply state identified on the gap gradient.
- [ ] Qualifies as a Prime Gap (heavily used, done badly) where possible.
- [ ] A free, credible path to the first users exists.
- [ ] A one-sentence wedge is clear.
- [ ] The gap is still fresh.
