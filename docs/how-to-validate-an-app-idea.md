# How to validate an app idea

Validating an app idea means answering one question before you write code: *is there proven demand for this job that existing software fails to meet, and can I reach the people who have it?* Most ideas die not because they were impossible to build, but because that question was never honestly answered.

This guide frames validation around the three things that actually decide it — demand, saturation, and execution gap — plus the factor that decides whether *you* can win: reachability. It pairs with the [opportunity score](opportunity-score.md) and the [gap gradient](gap-gradient.md).

---

## What "validated" really means

A validated idea is not one that sounds good or that friends say they would use. It is one where:

1. **Demand is real and current** — people already do this job and care.
2. **The market is not fully served** — existing tools leave a real gap.
3. **The execution gap is closable** — the failure is something you can fix.
4. **The users are reachable** — you can get in front of them affordably.

Validation is the process of finding evidence for each, *before* committing months of work. The order matters: there is no point assessing competitors for a job nobody wants.

---

## Step 1 — Validate demand first

The most common failure is building for demand that does not exist. So start here.

Look for **revealed demand**: evidence that people already perform the job and care enough to act. The strongest form is people actively using existing tools and complaining about them — they are paying with money or attention, which is the only demand that counts. Weak signals to distrust: "I would totally use that," your own excitement, and the absence of any existing solution (often a sign of no demand, not hidden opportunity).

If you cannot find people who already do this job and want it done better, stop. No later step can rescue a demand-less idea.

## Step 2 — Read the saturation honestly

Once demand is real, assess supply — but resist the binary. Place the task on the [gap gradient](gap-gradient.md):

- **Fully Served** — competitors exist and users are happy. This is the one state where "it already exists" really is bad news. Reconsider.
- **Lacks Features / Poorly Executed / Too Expensive** — competitors exist but users are unhappy. This is good news: demand is proven and the bar is low.
- **Undersupplied / Doesn't Exist** — thin or no supply. Softer competition, but you carry more of the burden of proving the market.

Remember: a crowded market is not disqualifying. Existing competitors with frustrated users are your best demand evidence.

## Step 3 — Size the execution gap

If apps exist, the deciding question is how badly they serve people. Read the actual complaints and ask:

- What do users **keep asking for** that nobody provides?
- What do they **hate about how** the tools work?
- What are they **refusing to pay** for?

The clearer and louder the gap, the stronger the validation — and the clearer your wedge. A vague "I could do it nicer" is not a validated gap. "Every top app in this category is missing X and reviewers mention it constantly" is.

## Step 4 — Confirm the gap is closable by you

A real, loud gap is only useful if you can close it. Some gaps persist because the missing feature is genuinely hard, requires data or partnerships you cannot get, or runs into platform limits. Verify that the thing users want is something you can actually build with the resources you have. A validated gap you cannot fill is not validated for you.

## Step 5 — Validate reachability

This is the step most idea checklists skip, and it is decisive for solo builders. Ask concretely: **how will the first hundred real users find this, without a marketing budget?**

- Do they **search** for a solution? Search and app-store discovery are open to anyone.
- Do they **congregate** in findable communities?
- Will they **browse** to it in an app store?

If the honest answer is "only by outspending incumbents on ads," the idea is not validated for an indie path, however real the gap. **Distribution, not the idea, is usually the binding constraint** — validate it like any other assumption.

## Step 6 — Pressure-test the willingness to pay

Demand to *use* is not the same as demand to *pay*. If your model depends on revenue, check that the frustration is painful enough that people already pay (even reluctantly) for inferior tools, or that the value is obviously worth a price. Free-but-annoying problems are real demand for usage and often poor demand for payment.

## Step 7 — Decide and move

Validation has a finish line. Once demand is proven, the gap is clear and closable, and users are reachable, further research is just delay — and gaps decay while you wait. Convert validation into a sharp wedge ("the one that finally does X" / "the one that actually works" / "the one you can afford") and start building before someone else fills the same gap.

---

## A validation checklist

- [ ] Stated as a single concrete task, not a category or a named app.
- [ ] Real, current demand from people already doing the job.
- [ ] Supply state placed on the gap gradient (and not Fully Served).
- [ ] Execution gap is specific and loud, not a vague "I could do it better."
- [ ] The gap is something you can actually build.
- [ ] A free, credible path to the first users exists.
- [ ] Evidence that the pain is worth paying for, if you need revenue.
- [ ] The gap is still fresh.

If most boxes are checked, the idea is validated enough to build. If demand or reachability is missing, no amount of polish will save it.
