# The gap gradient

The gap gradient is how Speculora describes the supply side of every software task. Instead of stamping a market "saturated" or "open," Speculora places each task on a spectrum of six supply states that capture *how* the job is currently served — and therefore how a new builder should enter, if at all.

This page explains each state, gives concrete examples, and defines the **Prime Gap** — the most valuable signal on the map. For shorter definitions see the [glossary](glossary.md); to see the gradient applied to real tasks, visit [speculora.com/gaps](https://speculora.com/gaps).

---

## Why a gradient instead of a label

A binary "saturated / not saturated" answer hides the only thing a builder cares about: whether the people in a market are well served. Two markets can both be "saturated" and be opposite opportunities — one full of beloved apps, the other full of overpriced, buggy ones that users tolerate because they have no choice.

The gap gradient separates **whether** a task is served from **how well** it is served. That second question is where almost all indie opportunity lives.

The six states run roughly from least to most open:

```
Fully Served  →  Lacks Features  →  Poorly Executed  →  Too Expensive  →  Undersupplied  →  Doesn't Exist
   (avoid)        (add feature)      (out-execute)        (undercut)       (soft comp.)     (greenfield)
```

Note that "more open" is not the same as "better opportunity." The far-right state, *Doesn't Exist*, is the riskiest, not the safest. The best opportunities usually cluster in the middle-left, where demand is proven but the incumbents are weak.

## The six supply states

### 1. Fully Served

**Definition:** Good apps already exist for the task, they have the features people want, and users are broadly satisfied.

**Signal:** Low complaint volume relative to usage; complaints are minor or about edge cases; high ratings across multiple credible apps.

**Example:** Reading e-books on a phone. Several mature, polished apps cover it well; most users are content.

**Entry strategy:** Generally avoid. Enter only with a strong, specific wedge — a segment the incumbents structurally cannot serve, or a genuinely new approach. Competing on "but nicer" against happy users rarely works.

---

### 2. Lacks Features

**Definition:** Apps for the task exist, but users repeatedly request capabilities that none of the incumbents provide.

**Signal:** A recurring chorus of the same feature requests across reviews of multiple apps — "I wish it could…", "still no support for…".

**Example:** A budgeting app that handles single accounts well but that users keep begging to support multiple shared accounts, or a specific bank, or a particular export format.

**Entry strategy:** Build the missing feature. The demand is already articulated for you. The risk is that an incumbent ships it first, so move while the request is loud and unmet.

---

### 3. Poorly Executed

**Definition:** The right features exist somewhere in the market, but the execution is bad — clunky interfaces, frequent crashes, slow performance, confusing flows, or dated design.

**Signal:** Complaints about *how* the app works rather than what it lacks — "constantly crashes," "the UI is a maze," "was great five years ago."

**Example:** A category leader that owns the features but is widely described as bloated, buggy, or stuck in an old design era, leaving users actively looking for an alternative.

**Entry strategy:** Out-execute. Same job, dramatically better experience. This is one of the most reliable indie plays because users are already primed to switch.

---

### 4. Too Expensive

**Definition:** The task is genuinely solved, but the available solutions are priced beyond a segment of people who still need the job done.

**Signal:** Complaints centered on price and paywalls — "why is basic functionality locked," "great app but I cannot justify the subscription," "looking for a cheaper alternative."

**Example:** A capable tool that gates everyday features behind an expensive recurring plan, frustrating casual or budget-conscious users who only need the core job.

**Entry strategy:** Undercut, or serve the priced-out segment with a leaner, cheaper, or one-time-purchase offering. Be deliberate about sustainability — competing on price requires a model that still works at the lower price.

---

### 5. Undersupplied

**Definition:** Real demand exists, but there are few players, or only weak ones.

**Signal:** Clear demand signal with a thin field of credible apps; the apps that exist are small, abandoned, or low quality.

**Example:** A specific, recurring workflow that many people clearly perform but for which the few available tools are obscure, unmaintained, or barely functional.

**Entry strategy:** Enter against soft competition. A competent, well-marketed entrant can take share without needing a dramatic differentiator — competence itself is the differentiator.

---

### 6. Doesn't Exist

**Definition:** There is a demand signal but no real solution yet. True greenfield.

**Signal:** People describe wanting the job done but reaching for workarounds, spreadsheets, manual processes, or stitched-together tools, because nothing purpose-built exists.

**Example:** An emerging, clearly expressed need that people currently solve by hand because no app addresses it directly.

**Entry strategy:** Greenfield — the highest upside and the highest risk. You must build the product *and* prove the market is willing to adopt and pay. The absence of competitors is not automatically good news; sometimes it means the market is not yet ready or not willing to pay.

## The Prime Gap

The **Prime Gap** is the single most valuable signal Speculora produces. It is the intersection of two conditions:

1. **Heavily used** — strong, durable, validated demand.
2. **Done badly** — the incumbents are weak: missing features, poor execution, or overpriced.

In gap-gradient terms, Prime Gaps live where high demand overlaps with *Lacks Features*, *Poorly Executed*, or *Too Expensive*. They are the most **disruptable** opportunities on the map because:

- The market is already proven — you do not have to manufacture demand.
- Users are actively unhappy — they are primed to switch.
- The bar to beat is low — the incumbents are weak by definition.

This is why Speculora highlights Prime Gaps above pure greenfield. A heavily-used task served badly is a far safer bet than an empty space where you would gamble on demand existing at all.

---

## How supply states change over time

Supply states are not permanent. A task in *Lacks Features* becomes *Fully Served* the moment an incumbent ships the missing feature. A *Poorly Executed* leader can rebuild and close its own gap. This is **gap decay**: opportunities shrink as the market reacts.

The practical implication: a gap is a time-sensitive asset. The freshness of the underlying signal matters, and the advantage goes to whoever moves a credible solution into the gap while it is still open. Speculora keeps its signal current precisely because a stale gap map is worse than none — it would point you at opportunities that have already closed.

---

## Putting it together

- Use the gradient to read *how* a market is served, not just whether it is.
- Prioritize Prime Gaps: heavily used and done badly.
- Treat *Doesn't Exist* as high-risk, not as a safe empty field.
- Remember that supply states decay, so act while the gap is fresh.
